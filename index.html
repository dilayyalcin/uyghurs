How the Turkish government - and its media - lost interest in the Uyghurs

Turkey regards the Uyghur people as ethnically and culturally related.

The 8th-century Uyghur Khaganate is commemorated as one of the "16 historical Turkish states," symbolised by a soldier in traditional costume standing behind President Recep Tayyip Erdogan during official ceremonies held for high-level visits.

In 2009, Erdogan described the alleged human rights abuses against China’s Uyghur population - including “mass surveillance, detention, indoctrination, and forced sterilisation” - as “nothing short of genocide”.

A decade later, in 2019, Turkey’s Foreign Ministry accused China of pursuing “systematic assimilation” of the Uyghurs.

However, a BBC Monitoring analysis shows that as Ankara has sought to deepen trade ties with Beijing, its public support for the Uyghur cause has diminished.

Once vocal criticism has been reduced to token references, limited to brief mentions in annual UN General Assembly speeches or occasional remarks during bilateral meetings.

All while Turkey saw historically high inflation rates and the two countries' trade surged.

Turkey’s state-run news agency Anadolu has mirrored this shift.

Its reporting on alleged Chinese abuses against Uyghurs fell from 30 stories in 2019 to just four in 2024.

State broadcaster TRT's reporting showed a similar trend.

Dropping from 10 reports on the issue in 2014 to zero in 2022 and 2023.
